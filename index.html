3-hour micro:bit challenge

The Challenge: create a programme for the micro:bit and write about it in 3-hours

At first, I really didn't know where to begin. I mean, I hadn't even seen a micro:bit before, let alone created a programme for one!

However, I had already reached this stage and had committed to completing the challenge. So, after panicking about what I couldn't do I actually began by looking up the micro:bit online and seeing what type of things I could programme - there were way more things than I expected; from games like "Rock Paper Scissors" to pH probes for science experiments.

What I decided

I was excited by the possibilities of what I could create and wanted to re-create "snake". However, I quickly realised that, with the 3-hour time limit and my limited coding experience, this was beyond my capabilities. In the end, I decided to programme a Magic 8 ball as it seemed challenging-yet-achievable.

Programming the Game

Again, I began by searching online to figure out how to programme the Magic 8 ball game and, luckily, managed to find a guide online. After reading the guide, the Magic 8 game seemed much more attainable than "snake" and I began.

The first task was to set up the "start" to prompt the player to ask a question.

Next, I selected the input "on shake" to instruct the micro:bit to respond to shaking. After this, I set a variable (named "Random") to pick a number 1 to 6 and used an "if" function to give different responses depending on the random number selected.

After this, the Magic 8 ball was complete. However, I still had some time left. So, I decided to add some animation to mimic "thinking" about the answer.

This was probably the most challenging part as it wasn't included in the guide.

First, I decided that I wanted to create an ellipsis kind of thinking gesture and programmed the micro:bit to "plot" a dot at (1,2) on the screen. Next, I added a "pause" for 500 ms, then added a second dot (and a pause), and then the third dot (and a pause). Rather than programming the dots again, I used the "repeat" function to repeat the motion 3 times and the thinking gesture was complete!

I also added a little surprise for anyone accidentally drops (or purposely throws) the game just for fun...

The Overall Experience

Overall, I really enjoyed learning more about the micro:bit and programming the game in the MakeCode Editor (it was much easier to use that I expected)!

I am grateful for what I have learned and glad that I completed the challenge, despite my initial fears!
